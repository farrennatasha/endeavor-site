HMS Endeavour · First Pacific Voyage

Beyond Captain Cook —
The Forgotten Crew

You've heard of Captain Cook. But 94 people sailed on the Endeavour. These are the scholars, navigators, artists, and ordinary sailors history left behind — many of them buried in waters far from home.

BataviaThe fatal stopover
October 1770 — The Endeavour docks in Batavia (now Jakarta, Indonesia) for repairs after nearly sinking on the Great Barrier Reef. Built on VOC marshland, the city is rife with malaria and dysentery. All but 10 of the 94 aboard fall ill. 30 people will die from Batavia's disease — in the port or at sea on the voyage home.
Died in Batavia

The Pacific Islanders — farthest from home

Tu
c.1725 – 1770
Died · Batavia
Art by Tupaia himself, 1769 · British Library · Public domain
High Priest · Navigator · Artist · Diplomat
Tupaia
Ra'iatea, Society Islands (now French Polynesia)
Celestial navigator Arioi priest Cartographer Cultural mediator

Tupaia was born into Polynesian nobility on Ra'iatea — an island so sacred it was considered the spiritual centre of the entire Pacific. He trained at Taputapuatea, the greatest marae (temple complex) in Polynesia, mastering celestial navigation: reading stars, ocean swells, wind patterns, migrating birds, and cloud formations to cross thousands of miles of open ocean without instruments. He was also an arioi — a member of an elite guild of priests, performers, and keepers of ancestral memory.

In 1757, Bora Boran warriors invaded Ra'iatea. Tupaia fled to Tahiti as a refugee, still bearing battle scars from a spear tipped with a stingray barb. He rebuilt his life as chief adviser to Purea, a powerful woman the British called the "Queen" of Tahiti. He was known across the islands as a genius — intellectually formidable, proud, and politically sharp. When the Endeavour arrived in 1769, he saw his chance to get back to sea.

→ Source: Te Ara NZ Biography

Tupaia navigated the Endeavour through the Pacific islands, personally diving to measure the depth of reef passes so Cook could sail safely. When the ship reached New Zealand, he discovered he could speak to the Māori — they shared ancient Polynesian roots. He became the voyage's essential translator and diplomat, preventing multiple violent confrontations. Cook wrote: "He was a Shrewd Sensible, Ingenious Man."

He also produced a remarkable map of 74+ Pacific islands using completely different logic to European cartography — encoding sailing routes, currents, and winds rather than fixed positions. Scholars are still decoding it today. He also drew and painted alongside Parkinson, producing some of the earliest indigenous perspectives on the European encounter. He amazed sailors by correctly pointing toward Tahiti even after the ship had sailed around New Zealand and up to Indonesia.

→ Source: Knowable Magazine (his map)
  • In 2019, navigators from Tahiti and Ra'ivavae sailed the ship Fa'afaite from Tahiti to New Zealand using only stars and ocean currents — in Tupaia's honour, marking the 250th anniversary of his voyage
  • His map is actively researched today by scholars at the University of Potsdam, Germany — a 2019 paper proposed a new way of reading it that changes everything
  • Major exhibitions at Te Papa Museum, Wellington, New Zealand celebrate him as one of the greatest navigators in human history
  • His drawings survive at the British Library and Natural History Museum London — some of the earliest Pacific art in existence
  • Buried on Damar Besar island near Jakarta — grave now lost
→ Source: Natural History Museum
The real leader of the voyage

Many Māori believed Tupaia — not Cook — was the leader of the expedition. He was a high priest from a sacred island their ancestors had left centuries ago. Banks wrote of him: "I do not know why I may not keep him as a curiosity, as well as some of my neighbours do lions and tigers." His contributions were essential. His credit was almost nothing. No portrait of him survives — only the drawings he made himself.

Ta
c.1757 – 1770
Died · Batavia
Engraving after Parkinson, 1773 · "The Lad Taiyota" · Public domain
Servant · Acolyte
Taiata
Society Islands (Tahiti / Ra'iatea) · age ~12 at departure
Youngest on the voyage Most forgotten Servant caste

Taiata was about 12 years old when the Endeavour left Tahiti in July 1769. He was Tupaia's servant and acolyte — described in ship records as being of the "tow-tow" (servant) caste, but noted by crew members for his remarkable intelligence and warmth. He boarded under the personal "protection" of Joseph Banks, who described Tupaia as a curiosity to collect — and Taiata came with him.

Almost nothing else is known about his background, his family, or his life before the voyage. He is one of the least documented people in the entire history of the Endeavour — a boy who crossed the Pacific and the Indian Ocean and died in a Dutch colonial port city at roughly 13 years old, thousands of miles from his home island. His background, birthdate, and full name are all unknown.

→ Source: Nat Waddell (dedicated article)

Ship records give almost no account of what Taiata specifically did on the voyage. He accompanied Tupaia as his personal attendant — present at every port, every cultural exchange, every negotiation. He would have been by Tupaia's side when the priest navigated, translated, and mediated. He likely assisted in small ways that were never recorded.

Sydney Parkinson drew his portrait during the voyage — one of the only deliberate acts of documentation anyone made about him personally. That drawing is now the only proof he existed.

  • His only surviving image — Parkinson's drawing titled "The Lad Taiyota, native of Otaheite, in the Dress of his Country" — is held at the British Museum, London
  • Buried with Tupaia on Damar Besar island, near Jakarta — grave now lost
  • No landmarks, memorials, or named places exist in his honour anywhere in the world
His last words

On December 17th, 1770 in Batavia, Taiata called out to those around him: "Tyua mate oee" — "My friends, I am dying." An eyewitness recorded that he died "like a patriarch, taking leave of us pathetically, each by name." He was roughly 13 years old. His death broke Tupaia completely — the high priest died just three days later.

Died in / after Batavia

The Scientists & Officers — they did the work, then died

SP
1745 – 1771
Died · at sea
Portrait · Natural History Museum London · Public domain
Botanical Artist
Sydney Parkinson
Edinburgh, Scotland · Quaker · age 23 at departure
1,300+ drawings First to draw eucalyptus First European artist in Australia

Born in Edinburgh in 1745 to a Quaker brewer, Parkinson showed an early gift for botanical drawing. After his father died young, the family moved to London where he worked drawing natural history specimens. He was known for his gentle demeanour, quiet diligence, and Quaker values — honest, hardworking, nonviolent. Banks heard of his precise, unromantic style and hired him first for a Newfoundland expedition, then offered him the Endeavour berth. He was 23 when the ship set sail.

He had no wealth, no connections, and no safety net. Banks hired him as a craftsman — everything he produced was owned by Banks. His brother Stanfield would later have to fight Banks legally just to publish Sydney's own journal after his death.

→ Source: State Library of New South Wales

Parkinson was hired to draw plants and animals, but when Alexander Buchan (the landscape artist) died in Tahiti, ALL artistic responsibilities fell to him alone. He drew nonstop — and at Botany Bay, specimens arrived so fast he couldn't finish paintings. He invented a personal method: detailed pencil outlines with colour notes in the margins to finish later. Banks's diary records he completed 94 sketches in just 14 days in May 1770. Crew members recalled him working through the night.

He made at least 1,300 drawings — 676 left unfinished. He was the first European to draw the eucalyptus, the kangaroo, and Aboriginal Australians from life. He also drew Taiata's portrait — the only image that proves the boy existed.

→ Source: Natural History Museum
  • 18 volumes of his botanical illustrations survive at the Natural History Museum London — 8 volumes depicting Australian flora alone
  • Banks's Florilegium — 743 engravings based on his drawings — was finally published 1980–1990, over 200 years after his death
  • Parkinson's Petrel (seabird) and Ficus parkinsonii (fig tree) are named in his honour
  • His journal — A Journal of a Voyage to the South Seas — was published by his brother in 1773 after a legal battle with Banks
→ Source: Australian National Botanic Gardens
Working against the clock

At Botany Bay, specimens arrived faster than Parkinson could finish them. He invented a personal shorthand — detailed outlines with colour notes — so he could return later. Of his 925 plant illustrations, 676 were never finished. He ran out of time and then, ultimately, life. The irony: his unfinished sketches became some of the most important botanical records in history. Banks owned all of them.

HS
1733 – 1771
Died · at sea
No known portrait exists
Naturalist · Secretary · Artist · Former Surgeon
Herman Spöring Jr.
Turku, Finland (then Swedish Empire) · age 35 at departure
Most invisible crew member Watchmaker turned scientist Saved the Transit of Venus

Born in Turku in 1733, the son of a professor of medicine at the Royal Academy of Åbo, Spöring trained as a surgeon under his father. Around 1755, at 22, he moved to London — and started working at a watchmaker's shop. This career pivot is one of the most fascinating details of the voyage: a trained surgeon fixing timepieces in London. Through London's scientific circles he met Daniel Solander, who hired him as a personal clerk.

By 1768, Banks recruited him as secretary, naturalist, and artist for the Endeavour voyage. He was fluent in multiple languages, skilled in surgery, precision instrument repair, technical drawing, and natural history classification — and received almost no individual credit for any of it. His birthdate, portrait, and grave location are all unknown.

→ Source: Wikipedia (biographical overview)

When the voyage's astronomical quadrant — the instrument needed to observe the Transit of Venus — was stolen and damaged in Tahiti, Spöring, the ex-watchmaker, repaired it. Without him, the official scientific mission of the entire voyage would have failed. He also produced detailed technical drawings of Māori carvings, tools, and canoes in New Zealand — among the earliest systematic records of Māori material culture.

He died of dysentery at sea after Batavia, on the same passage home that killed Parkinson and Green, in January 1771. He was about 37 years old.

  • Cook named a small island after him at Tolaga Bay, New Zealand — now known by its Māori name Pourewa
  • One dedicated academic paper exists about him, published in 2000 — still the least researched person on the entire voyage
  • No portrait, no named memorial, no genus or species named after him — despite his contributions being essential
→ Source: ResearchGate (research paper)
The man who did everything, credited for nothing

Spöring is listed in most accounts simply as "Banks's secretary." But he was simultaneously a trained surgeon, a watchmaker precise enough to repair astronomical instruments, a naturalist, and a draughtsman. He did the work of four people — and because Banks owned everything produced in his employ, almost none of Spöring's contributions are attributed to him individually. He is the most under-researched person on the entire voyage.

CG
1734 – 1771
Died · at sea
Portrait of Charles Green · Alchetron / Wikipedia · Public domain
Royal Astronomer
Charles Green
Swinton, Yorkshire, England · farmer's son turned Greenwich astronomer
Transit of Venus Royal Observatory Greenwich The reason the voyage existed

Born in 1734 in Swinton, Yorkshire, the youngest son of a farmer, Green rose entirely through merit — unusual for the era. He became assistant to Nevil Maskelyne, the Astronomer Royal at the prestigious Royal Observatory in Greenwich. His specialty was precision astronomical measurement, particularly the observation of planetary transits to calculate cosmic distances.

He was paid 200 guineas for the voyage — double Cook's astronomical salary — reflecting how seriously the Royal Society took his mission. He was about 34 when the ship set sail, a quiet, technically brilliant man who seems to have struggled with the social demands of a long sea voyage. Cook's later journal suggests Green drank heavily on the voyage home from Batavia, possibly as his health deteriorated.

→ Source: Natural History Museum

Green's mission was to observe the Transit of Venus from Tahiti — the rare passage of Venus across the face of the sun, critical for calculating the distance between the Earth and the Sun. He successfully observed it on June 3, 1769, from Fort Venus in Tahiti, alongside Cook. He also observed the Transit of Mercury from New Zealand in November 1769 — a bonus achievement. His precise longitude measurements throughout the voyage were later praised by 19th century astronomers.

He died on January 29, 1771 — 12 days out from Batavia — reportedly having climbed out of his bunk delirious and put his legs through the porthole during the night.

  • Green Island off the Queensland coast, Australia — named after him by Cook, still exists as a tourist destination today
  • His Transit of Venus observations were published posthumously in the Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society, 1771 — the most prestigious science journal in the world
  • His wife received a pension of £50/year from the Royal Society — then nothing after the voyage was officially concluded
The voyage existed because of him

The Endeavour's official mission — the reason the Royal Society funded it, the reason Cook was commissioned — was to observe the Transit of Venus. This was Green's assignment. Without it, there was no voyage. Cook's secret orders to find and claim Australia would never have been issued. Green is the man whose mission created the conditions for everything that followed — and he is remembered almost nowhere.

ZH
1739 – 1771
Died · at sea
No known portrait exists
Second Lieutenant · Cook's Second-in-Command
Zachary Hicks
Stepney, London · first to sight mainland Australia
First to sight Australia Tuberculosis entire voyage Saved Cook's life

Born in Stepney, East London in 1739, Hicks likely enlisted or was pressed into naval service as a young man. He earned every rank he held — serving with the East India Company, then the Royal Navy, before being commissioned as second lieutenant to Cook on May 26, 1768. He was described by historian J.C. Beaglehole as "a man of ability" but without "the chance to shine — perhaps born to be a lieutenant."

Crucially, Hicks had tuberculosis before the voyage even began. Cook later wrote in his journal that Hicks had "a Consumption of which he had been ill the whole voyage, so that in fact he was dying from the day we left England." He carried this illness silently across the entire Pacific — still performing his duties, still leading shore parties, still in command of the ship whenever Cook went ashore.

→ Source: Royal Society (his journal)

Whenever Cook was ashore, Hicks was in charge of the ship. He led shore parties in Rio de Janeiro, was held hostage by Portuguese authorities during a diplomatic confrontation, commanded the recovery of deserters in Tahiti, and made the expedition's first sustained contact with Indigenous Australians at Botany Bay — even recording some of their words.

On April 19, 1770, on his morning watch, Hicks was the first person on the Endeavour to sight mainland Australia. Cook named the landing point after him. In New Zealand, when Māori attacked the shore party, Hicks's quick thinking saved the lives of Cook, Banks, and Solander. He died at sea on May 26, 1771 — the exact third anniversary of his commission to the Endeavour.

  • Point Hicks, Victoria, Australia — the exact spot where he first sighted Australia. A lighthouse and monument stand there today, with an inscription naming him
  • Hicks Bay, East Coast New Zealand — named after him, a small coastal town today
  • Zachary Hicks Crescent, Endeavour Hills, Melbourne — a street named after him in a suburb named after the ship
  • His journal survives at the National Archives, Kew — 174 densely handwritten folios covering almost the entire voyage
→ Source: Royal Society (his journal)
Dying the whole voyage

Cook wrote in his log: "He had been ill the whole voyage of a Consumption of which he lingered during the whole of it, so that in fact he was dying from the day we left England." Hicks performed every duty, led every mission, and stayed on watch — for three years — while dying of tuberculosis. He was 31 when he died at sea, buried off the island of St Helena. He never got to see Australia from the shore.

JM
both d. 1770–71
Died · Batavia & at sea

Ship's Surgeon · Midshipman
The Munkhouse Brothers
William & Jonathan · Penrith, Cumberland, England
Two brothers One saved the ship Both died on the voyage

William Brougham Munkhouse was the ship's surgeon — described by contemporaries as disorganised and prone to drink, but well educated and a superlative observer. He was one of the senior officers on the ship, assigned a private cabin on the rear lower deck alongside the lieutenants. He was the older of the two brothers.

Jonathan Munkhouse was a midshipman — a trainee officer — on the same voyage as his brother. He was younger, more disciplined, and it was he who would have the most consequential moment of the entire voyage. The two brothers from Penrith in Cumberland had both ended up on the same ship, crossing the other side of the world together — and neither would come home.

→ Source: Captain Cook Society (Munkhouse brothers)

William's most famous connection: His 11-year-old servant, Nicholas Young, was the first person to sight land on the approach to New Zealand — Cook gave the boy a gallon of rum and named the headland "Young Nick's Head." William died in Batavia on November 5, 1770 — one of the very first to die in the port itself, before the ship even departed.

Jonathan's defining moment: When the Endeavour struck the Great Barrier Reef and was taking on water fast — the entire crew faced death — Jonathan proposed "fothering": sewing oakum and wool into an old sail and drawing it under the ship's hull, letting water pressure force it into the hole. He had seen it done once on a merchant ship. Cook trusted him. It worked. The ship was saved. Jonathan died at sea on February 6, 1771, just weeks after leaving Batavia — the ship he saved carried on without him.

  • Nicholas Young, William's 11-year-old servant who first sighted New Zealand, survived the voyage and went on to work for Banks — a bronze statue of "Young Nick" stands at Waikanae Beach, Gisborne, New Zealand today
  • Jonathan's fothering technique directly saved the Endeavour — without it, the entire voyage ends on the Great Barrier Reef in 1770 and none of the scientific work returns to England
  • Both brothers' wills and journals survive in New Zealand and UK archives
The brothers who sailed together and died together

William and Jonathan Munkhouse left their home in Penrith, Cumberland together and sailed to the other side of the world on the same ship. William — the brilliant surgeon — died first, in Batavia on November 5, 1770. Jonathan — who helped save the ship on the Great Barrier Reef — died at sea on February 6, 1771. Neither made it home. The Captain Cook Society records their surname as "Munkhouse."

Survived

The Survivors — they got all the glory

JB
1743 – 1820
Survived
Joshua Reynolds, 1773 · National Portrait Gallery · Public domain
Naturalist · Patron · President of the Royal Society
Joseph Banks
London, England · inherited a fortune at 21
£6,000/year at age 21 42 years President of Royal Society Shaped modern Australia

Born in London in 1743, Banks inherited Revesby Abbey (a grand Lincolnshire estate) and an income of £6,000 a year — roughly £1 million today — at just 21. He was wealthy enough to never work a day in his life. Instead, he threw himself into natural history. At Eton and Christ Church, Oxford, he was reportedly so bored by standard curriculum that he paid a botanical lecturer to teach him privately.

He was 25 when the Endeavour sailed. He personally paid a large sum to bring himself and eight others — Solander, Spöring, Parkinson, Buchan, two other artists, and two Black servants. He brought 150 books, microscopes, and telescopes. He was energetic, socially brilliant, and politically connected. He survived Batavia without the severe illness that killed many around him, returned to England a celebrity, and lived to 77.

→ Source: Natural History Museum

Banks collected over 30,000 specimens during the voyage, including 1,300 species new to Western science. He was the expedition's financier, chief naturalist, and social engine. He built relationships with Tahitians by learning their language, commissioned Tupaia to join the ship, and drove the pace of scientific collection at every stop.

After the voyage, he was elected President of the Royal Society in 1778 and held the position for 42 years. He advised King George III directly on science and colonial policy, championed Botany Bay as the site for British settlement, and shaped Kew Gardens into a global botanical research hub. When the voyage returned, the press initially called it "the Banks and Solander expedition" — Cook went unnamed in several articles.

  • Kew Gardens, London — still one of the world's most important botanical research institutions, Banks shaped its direction. Open to the public today
  • Bankstown, Sydney — one of Sydney's major suburbs named after him
  • The genus Banksia — iconic Australian flowering plants — named after him; over 170 species
  • His recommendation directly led to the First Fleet sailing to Botany Bay in 1788, establishing the British penal colony that became modern Sydney
  • Banks's Florilegium — 34 volumes of engravings based on Parkinson's drawings — published 1980–1990, over 200 years after the voyage
→ Source: Natural History Museum
The collector of people

Banks wrote of Tupaia: "I do not know why I may not keep him as a curiosity, as well as some of my neighbours do lions and tigers." When his two Black servants died of hypothermia in South America, he noted it with barely a sentence. When his greyhound died on the voyage home, he was overcome with grief. He survived Batavia, claimed ownership of all scientific work everyone else produced, and died at 77 surrounded by his collections. The people he brought with him were less fortunate.

DS
1733 – 1782
Survived
Lithograph by Miss J Turner · Wellcome Collection · CC BY 4.0
Botanist · Taxonomist · Disciple of Linnaeus
Daniel Solander
Piteå, Sweden · sent to London at 27, never went back
Linnaeus's favourite student Founded NHM collections Invented Solander box

Born in 1733 in Piteå, a small town in northern Sweden, Solander enrolled at Uppsala University in 1750 and quickly became the star pupil of Carl Linnaeus — the man who invented the binomial nomenclature system, the way every living thing on Earth is scientifically named. Linnaeus sent him to London in 1759 as an emissary to spread the new classification system. Solander initially intended to complete his doctorate and return. He never did.

He became Assistant Librarian at the British Museum in 1762, cataloguing natural history collections. He met Banks in 1764, and the friendship changed both their lives. He was known as warm, sociable, and intellectually generous — the kind of person who made science feel like conversation. He got malaria in Batavia, survived, was confined to his room for two weeks in South Africa recovering, and made it home.

→ Source: Natural History Museum (detailed article)

Solander was the scientific backbone of Banks's operation. He meticulously catalogued every specimen using Linnaeus's system, writing descriptions on "Solander slips" — small handwritten sheets that still survive today in bound volumes at the Natural History Museum. He contributed to describing over 1,300 new species from the voyage. He and Banks collected around 30,000 specimens total.

After the voyage, he became Keeper of Natural History at the British Museum in 1773. He and Banks planned to publish their findings — 700 printing plates were engraved — but neither completed it before they died. The Florilegium was finally published in the 1980s. He died at Banks's home on May 13, 1782, of a cerebral haemorrhage, aged 49. He was buried in the Swedish Church in London.

  • Natural History Museum, London — his collections and cataloguing work are the founding core of the entire museum, still one of the world's greatest today
  • Cape Solander, Botany Bay, Australia — the exact landing point of the Endeavour, now a famous whale-watching spot
  • Solander Islands, New Zealand — named after him
  • The Solander box — his clamshell protective case for manuscripts — still used in museum and library archives worldwide today
  • In 2017, a garden was named in his honour at the Swedish Embassy in Canberra, Australia, featuring plants he collected in 1770
→ Source: Natural History Museum
The man who turned everything down

Solander was offered the top post at the Academy of Sciences in St Petersburg. He was offered Carl Linnaeus's own professorship at Uppsala — to become the successor to the father of modern taxonomy. He turned both down. Linnaeus was furious and never forgave him. Solander chose London, Banks, and his work — and never returned to Sweden. He died at Banks's house, having lived there for years. He was presented with an honorary doctorate by King George III in 1771 — but never completed his actual Swedish doctorate he left behind in 1759.
